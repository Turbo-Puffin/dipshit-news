Eighteen trillion. With a *T.* That's the number the President quoted at a Small Business Summit at the White House this week, describing the level of investment his administration has secured. Folks in the room nodded. Cameras rolled. He repeated it.

Now, eighteen trillion is a real number. It is also, conveniently, almost double what his own administration's tracker actually lists. As of late 2025, that tracker shows about $9.6 trillion. Of that, Bloomberg's economics desk took the time to do the math nobody at the podium did. Their finding: about $7 trillion in genuine pledged investment. The other $2.6 trillion are agreements to *buy stuff* — mostly natural gas. That is not investment. That is shopping.

So $7 trillion in actual capital commitments. The President said eighteen.

I have been tending bar to gentlemen who exaggerated their hand for thirty years. The pattern is the same. The number gets bigger every time the story gets told. By next month it'll be twenty. By Christmas it'll be fifty. By the next State of the Union it will exceed the global economy and somebody at the network desk will still nod and say "well, that's what the President is reporting." Buddy. *Y'all charge fourteen dollars for a sandwich.* You can do the math.

Here's the test for whether a number is real: somebody, somewhere, can show you the receipts. The administration's own dashboard shows the receipts. The receipts say nine point six. They print it. In their own font.

When the man at the podium says eighteen, and the dashboard he commissioned says nine point six, you have not caught a man being inaccurate. You have caught a man not bothering to read his own homework.

That ought to concern you.
