Fifty-two to forty-seven. That is the procedural vote, on Thursday afternoon, by which the United States Senate advanced a resolution that would require the President to obtain congressional authorization before further military action in Venezuela. Five Republicans voted with the Democrats. Senators Paul, Murkowski, Collins, Young, and Hawley.

This is a slim margin and a procedural one. The resolution must clear several further steps and survive a House and a veto. The headline event of the morning is the alignment, the principle of the vote rather than its operative effect.

The Constitution, in the version of it that has been in force since 1789, vests the authority to declare war in the Congress. The War Powers Resolution of 1973, which passed over a presidential veto, is the operative statute. It permits a president to commit forces in defense of the country for sixty days without authorization. It does not permit a president to capture a head of state, hold him on a Navy ship, post a photograph, and expand operations.

That last clause is what the resolution addresses. The resolution does not contest the events of January 3. It contests the events of January 4 and onward.

The President responded on Truth Social with a post calling the five Republicans names and predicting that they "should never be elected to office again." This is a notable phrasing, because four of the five are not up for re-election in 2026, and the fifth, the senator from Missouri, is. The senator from Missouri has cast votes against the President before. He has continued to win.

A constitutional system of government depends, structurally, on a certain number of senators behaving as senators rather than as employees. Five is not the number that constitutes a check. Five is the number that demonstrates a check exists, in latency, available to be activated by a sufficient pressure event.

Caracas was a sufficient pressure event for five. The events that follow will determine the number for which the next vote is sufficient.

Calmly documenting the decline.
